REFRAME

Three Reframes

Using our journal data and the audit, we developed three distinct ways to reframe the problem, each approached from a different angle.

Mindset Reframe

Get rid of all-or-nothing thinking

Unhealthy eating habits are sustained by shame and the pressure to be perfect. Build habits incrementally, with no pressure to be perfect.

Environment Reframe

Frame 2: You don't need a fancy kitchen

People are failing because their environment sets them up to fail. Cook within your actual constraints, not an assumed ideal.

Identity Reframe

Frame 3: I'm not a cook

The perceived learning curve keeps people from ever starting. Build confidence through shared cooking experiences and community.

Reframe

Frame Validation

To test which frame resonated most, we presented all three to 10 participants and asked them to respond to each - rating their gut reactions, voting on what resonated most with their lived experience, and dot voting across all three frames.

Grouping feedback across participants regardless of which frame they referenced, several patterns and design principles formed:

Finding 1

Variability

Kitchens, tools, and ingredients change constantly. People aren't failing to cook - their conditions keep shifting.

"I moved a lot so the condition of the kitchen I could have were always changing."

Principle 1

Flexibility

Support multiple ways to complete the same meal based on what you actually have - not what a recipe assumes.

Finding 2

Overwhelm

The biggest cooking barrier is mental overload - too much information, too many steps, all at once.

"I get overwhelmed trying to follow a recipe while managing heat, timing, and everything else."

Principle 2

Pacing

Address and guide through uncontrollable external factors - give people more room to breathe and alleviate cognitive load.

Finding 3

Pressure

Cooking feels easier when it's low-pressure and forgiving. The expectation to do it "right" is the real barrier.

"I just want it to feel like it doesn't matter if I mess up - that's when I actually try."

Principle 3

Low-stakes

Non-committal and low-stakes allows relief from pressure and expectation - to cook the "right" way.

Feature Concept 3

Configure your kitchen

People's kitchens are never the same twice. By defining their limitations upfront, users only ever see recipes they can actually make.

Browse recipes matched to you

Low effort browsing built around your kitchen profile. The match percentage takes the thinking out of it.

Create a substitute recipe

The most direct answer to the reframe. People are already doing this manually - searching for workarounds when a recipe assumes equipment they don't have. This feature does it for them.

Takeaways

What I'd do differently:

Name the target user earlier and make the design audit gap explicit on the slide - not just in the room. Insights need to live on the page.

What this project taught me:

The hardest part of reframing isn't coming up with the new frame. It's having the discipline to let the evidence make the case for you.

What I want to carry forward:

A reframe only works if the audience arrives at it themselves. Your job is to build the path.
